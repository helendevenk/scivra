Eruption Type Notes

Shield Volcano: Basaltic magma (45–52% SiO₂), very low viscosity (~10 Pa·s), low gas pressure. Effusive eruptions — lava flows spread widely. Broad, gentle slope <10°. Examples: Mauna Loa (9 km tall from seafloor), Hawaiian chain.

Stratovolcano / Composite: Andesitic-rhyolitic (52–72% SiO₂), high viscosity (10⁶–10¹⁰ Pa·s), trapped gas pressure → explosive. Steep cone 30–40°. VEI 4–6 typical. Examples: Mt. St. Helens (1980, VEI 5), Pinatubo (1991, VEI 6), Krakatau.

Cinder Cone: Basaltic-andesitic scoria, small, steep (30–40°), short-lived eruptions. Max height ~300 m. Example: Paricutín, Mexico (born 1943, grew 424 m in 1 year).

Knowledge Cards
VEI Scale
Volcanic Explosivity Index: logarithmic (each step = 10× more material). VEI 0: gentle effusion. VEI 5: Mt. St. Helens 1980 (1 km³). VEI 7: Tambora 1815 (caused "Year Without a Summer"). VEI 8: Yellowstone supervolcano (last ~640,000 yr ago).
Pyroclastic Flow
Dense, fast-moving current of hot gas and volcanic matter (700–1000°C) traveling at 100–700 km/h. Most deadly volcanic hazard. Pompeii buried by Vesuvius 79 CE pyroclastic surge.
Lahar
Volcanic mudflow: water (rain or melted snow/ice) mixes with volcanic ash and debris. Can travel 100+ km down river valleys. 1985 Nevado del Ruiz lahar killed 23,000 people in Armero, Colombia.
Why It Matters

Volcanic eruptions affect millions: 800 million people live within 100 km of historically active volcanoes. Understanding eruption type from magma composition and geophysical monitoring enables accurate hazard zonation, evacuation planning, and aviation safety (ash cloud prediction). Large eruptions temporarily cool climate by injecting SO₂ into the stratosphere.
