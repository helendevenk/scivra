Heat Engines & Thermodynamics — AP Physics

Core Formulas
Carnot efficiency — theoretical maximum for any heat engine between T_H and T_C (in Kelvin)
First Law applied to a cycle: net work W = Q_H − Q_C, heat absorbed minus heat rejected
Coefficient of Performance is different from efficiency — used for refrigerators and heat pumps
Second Law (Clausius inequality): total entropy of an isolated system never decreases
Concept

A heat engine absorbs heat Q_H from a hot reservoir, converts part of it into useful work W, and rejects the remainder Q_C to a cold reservoir. The Carnot engine is the idealized version: perfectly reversible, no friction, isothermal and adiabatic processes only.

The Second Law of Thermodynamics sets an absolute ceiling on efficiency. No real engine can beat the Carnot efficiency η = 1 − T_C/T_H. Real engines (steam, internal combustion) run 15–40% efficient; the rest is waste heat.

In the P-V diagram, the area enclosed by the cycle equals net work done per cycle. Isothermal curves are hyperbolas (PV = const), while adiabatic curves are steeper (PV^γ = const).

Knowledge Cards

Sadi Carnot (1824)

Carnot published his landmark paper at age 28, before the First Law of Thermodynamics was even formally stated. He proved the efficiency limit using a thought experiment about reversible engines — a stunning feat of abstract reasoning.

Common Misconception

Higher efficiency does NOT mean more power. A very slow Carnot engine approaches theoretical efficiency but produces tiny power. Real engines trade efficiency for practical output rates.

AP Connection

AP Physics 2 expects you to calculate Carnot efficiency, identify which process is isothermal vs adiabatic on a P-V diagram, and apply the First Law to closed cycles (net ΔU = 0 over one full cycle).

Why It Matters
Power plants (coal, nuclear, gas) are heat engines — Carnot efficiency dictates how much fuel is wasted as waste heat.
Car engines run ~25–35% efficient; the rest heats the air and coolant. Improving this by a few percent saves billions of gallons of fuel.
Refrigerators and heat pumps are heat engines run in reverse — same physics, different direction of energy flow.
