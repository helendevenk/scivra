Concept

A hanging mass stretches the spring until the spring force exactly balances gravity — that is the equilibrium point (yellow dashed line). Displace the mass from there and release it: it oscillates symmetrically about equilibrium.

The key AP insight: T = 2π√(m/k) contains no g. On the Moon (g = 1.62 m/s²) the equilibrium is much closer to the ceiling — less stretch — but the period is identical to Earth. Gravity shifts where you oscillate, not how fast.

This follows from the math: when you measure displacement from equilibrium, the gravitational PE and extra spring PE cancel out, and you recover pure SHM with the same restoring constant k/m.

KEY MISCONCEPTION

REAL-WORLD APPLICATION

NASA measures astronaut body mass on the ISS using the Body Mass Measurement Device — an oscillating spring chair — because T = 2π√(m/k) works perfectly without gravity.

AP EXAM STRATEGY

AP Physics 1 free-response frequently compares spring-mass systems on different planets. Template answer: "same T because T = 2π√(m/k) is independent of g; different x_eq because x_eq = mg/k."

Why It Matters
  • Seismographs use spring-mass systems to detect ground motion — gravity variation across locations doesn't affect their calibration
  • MEMS accelerometers in smartphones contain microscopic spring-mass oscillators sensing 1–5 g forces
  • Bridge and building resonance analysis requires knowing the natural oscillation frequency — k and m, not g
