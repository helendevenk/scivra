Concept

Static friction is a reactive force — it matches the applied force exactly until it reaches its maximum. At that point the box "breaks loose" and kinetic friction takes over, which is slightly lower. This transition is the key insight of this lab.

Why is μₛ > μₖ? At the microscopic level, surfaces in static contact have more atomic interlocking than surfaces already sliding. Once motion starts, the contact time per asperity drops, reducing adhesion.

Watch the box color: blue = static, orange = sliding. The force-bar at the bottom shows how close you are to the breakaway point.

Knowledge Cards
The Stribeck Curve
In engineering, friction actually varies with sliding speed. The simplified Coulomb model (constant μₖ) is a good AP-level approximation.
Real μ Values
Rubber on dry concrete: μₛ ≈ 0.8. Polished ice on ice: μₛ ≈ 0.03–0.1. Steel on steel (dry): μₛ ≈ 0.6.
AP Connection
AP Physics 1 Learning Objective 2.B.1: characterize forces by the direction and magnitude of the interaction.
Why It Matters

• Anti-lock braking systems (ABS) keep tires near the static→kinetic transition to maximize deceleration.

• Rock climbing shoe rubber is engineered for maximum μₛ on granite and limestone.

• Understanding friction transitions is essential for AP Physics 1 incline and pulley problems.

About Friction Forces Lab

Explore the transition from static to kinetic friction.

  • Static state: friction = applied force (reactive)
  • Breakaway: F_applied > μₛ × N → motion starts
  • Kinetic state: friction = μₖ × N (constant)
  • g = 9.8 m/s², N = mg on flat surface
