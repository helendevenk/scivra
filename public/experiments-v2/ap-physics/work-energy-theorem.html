Work-Energy Theorem
W_net = ΔKE · AP Physics 1
Core Formulas
Net work equals the change in kinetic energy
Work = force × displacement (when parallel)
Friction force opposes motion
Net force accelerates the block via Newton's 2nd law
Concept

The Work-Energy Theorem says: the net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy. Applied force does positive work; friction does negative work. Their sum is the net work, which equals ΔKE.

Watch the block slide: the KE bar grows as the applied force pushes it. When friction is high, it fights back — less net work, smaller KE gain. This is Newton's 2nd law reframed in energy terms.

Unlike energy conservation, this theorem works even when energy is lost to heat (friction). It tracks only kinetic energy, not total energy.

Common Misconception
Friction doesn't just "slow things down" — it converts kinetic energy into thermal energy. Energy is still conserved; it just changes form.
AP Connection
AP Physics 1 Big Idea 3: Interactions between objects can transfer energy. The work-energy theorem is the bridge between force (dynamics) and energy (thermodynamics).
Why It Matters
▸Car braking distance is calculated using the work-energy theorem: W_friction = ΔKE
▸Crumple zones in cars maximize stopping distance to reduce the net force (work is spread over more displacement)
▸Elevators, conveyor belts, and all motors are designed using W = F·d calculations
