Kepler's Three Laws

Law 1: Planets follow elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.
Law 2: A line from planet to Sun sweeps equal areas in equal time intervals.
Law 3: The square of orbital period is proportional to the cube of semi-major axis.

Concept Explanation

Kepler's laws describe how planets move under gravity. An ellipse has two foci — the Sun sits at one focus, not the center. Eccentricity e controls how stretched the ellipse is (e=0 is a circle).

Equal-area sweeping (Law 2) is a direct consequence of conservation of angular momentum. A planet speeds up near perihelion and slows near aphelion — the swept area stays constant.

Law 3 connects orbital geometry to dynamics. It allowed astronomers to compute relative distances in the Solar System long before spacecraft existed.

Key Facts

Historical Note: Kepler published Laws 1 & 2 in 1609, Law 3 in 1619 — 10 years before Newton was born. Newton later proved they follow from universal gravitation.
Common Misconception: Earth's seasons are NOT caused by varying distance from the Sun. Earth's orbit is nearly circular (e=0.017). Seasons come from axial tilt.
AP Connection: Law 3 ties directly to circular orbit derivation: set gravitational force = centripetal force → T² ∝ r³.

Why It Matters

  • Satellite orbit design (GPS, ISS)
  • Exoplanet detection via transit timing
  • Interplanetary mission planning (Hohmann transfers)
  • AP Physics C: Universal Gravitation — core topic
