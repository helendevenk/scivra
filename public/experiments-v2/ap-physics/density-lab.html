Density Lab Archimedes Principle · Buoyancy

Core Formulas

Density is mass per unit volume — the single most important property for floating/sinking

Archimedes: buoyant force equals weight of displaced fluid

Object floats when ρ_obj ≤ ρ_fluid; fraction submerged = ρ_obj / ρ_fluid

Concept

Density tells you how much mass is packed into a given volume. An object floats if it is less dense than the fluid — buoyancy is the upward force of the displaced fluid. The key insight is that an object only needs to displace fluid equal to its own weight, not its volume.

When an object is fully submerged, buoyant force = ρ_fluid × V_obj × g. If this exceeds the object's weight (ρ_obj × V_obj × g), the net force is upward and it floats. If not, it sinks. The ratio ρ_obj/ρ_fluid tells you exactly how much is submerged.

The displacement method (Archimedes) lets you measure density indirectly: weigh an object in air, then in fluid. The weight difference equals the buoyant force = ρ_fluid × V_displaced × g. Solve for V, then ρ = m/V.

🔬 Archimedes' Eureka
Archimedes discovered buoyancy (~250 BCE) when he stepped into a bath and noticed the water level rise. He realised displaced volume equals object volume — a way to measure irregular shapes.
⚠️ Common Misconception
Steel ships float not because steel is light, but because the hollow hull displaces a huge volume — the average density of ship + air is less than water.

Why It Matters

  • • Ship and submarine design: control buoyancy by adjusting ballast water
  • • Medical: blood density measurement diagnoses anemia and dehydration
  • • Geology: less dense continental crust floats on denser mantle (isostasy)
