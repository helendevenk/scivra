Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

Maximum kinetic energy of ejected electron. hf = photon energy, φ = work function.
Threshold frequency below which no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity.
Stopping voltage needed to halt the fastest electrons. Measures KE_max directly.
Planck's constant h = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s, or in eV: h = 4.136×10⁻¹⁵ eV·s

Concept Explanation

Classical wave theory predicted that any frequency of light, given enough intensity, would eventually eject electrons. Experiments showed this was completely wrong: below a threshold frequency, no electrons emerge no matter how intense the light.

Einstein's 1905 insight: light comes in discrete packets (photons) with energy E = hf. A single photon must have enough energy to overcome the work function φ in one interaction. More intensity just means more photons per second — more electrons ejected — but not faster ones.

The stopping voltage V₀ = KE_max/e provides a direct, precise way to measure h experimentally. Millikan's 1916 experiment confirmed Einstein's equation perfectly.

Key Facts

Nobel Prize: Einstein won the 1921 Nobel Prize for this discovery — not for Relativity! It established the particle nature of light.
Misconception: Increasing intensity does NOT increase electron speed. It only increases the number of electrons emitted. KE_max depends only on frequency.
AP Connection: This experiment is direct evidence for wave-particle duality — a photon must be a particle to interact with one electron in a single collision.

Why It Matters

  • Solar cells (photovoltaic effect)
  • Digital cameras (CCD sensors)
  • Night-vision devices
  • X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS)
