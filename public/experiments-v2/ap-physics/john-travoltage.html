Static Electricity & Discharge · AP Physics C
Core Formulas

Surface charge Q on a conductor raises its potential V proportionally.

Electric field at body surface depends on total charge and geometry.

Energy released in a spark discharge equals ½QV stored in the body.

How It Works

When John rubs his foot on the carpet, the triboelectric effect transfers electrons from the carpet to his shoe sole, leaving his body with a net negative charge. This builds up potential across his body — a conductor.

When his hand approaches a grounded metal doorknob, the strong electric field at the air gap exceeds air's dielectric breakdown strength (~3 MV/m). The air ionizes, forming a conductive plasma channel — a spark. Charge rushes through it in microseconds, equalizing potential to zero.

High humidity dramatically reduces buildup: water molecules are polar and bleed charge off the body continuously, preventing large charge accumulation.

Knowledge Cards
⚡ The human body capacitance is roughly 100–200 pF. Even a small voltage of 20 kV stores ~0.02–0.04 mJ — enough for a painful spark!
⚠️ Common misconception: static discharge doesn't require touch. Sparks jump across several millimeters of air at typical body voltages.
🔗 AP Connection: Gauss's Law explains why charge distributes on the conductor surface; Coulomb's Law explains the force that drives spark formation.
Why It Matters
  • ESD (Electrostatic Discharge) destroys microchips — antistatic wristbands ground engineers to prevent it
  • Lightning rods work on the same principle: sharp conductors bleed charge to prevent explosive discharge
  • Fuel handlers use static-dissipating shoes to prevent igniting vapors
