Concept

Curve fitting is the art of finding the mathematical function that best describes a noisy dataset. The goal isn't a perfect fit — it's the simplest model that captures the underlying physics.

R² measures how much of the data's variance is explained by the model. R²=1 is a perfect fit; R²=0 means the model is no better than a flat horizontal line. RMSE gives the average prediction error in the same units as y.

Drag data points to update the fit in real time. Watch how R² changes — and notice that adding polynomial terms always improves fit, but may not improve understanding (overfitting).

Knowledge Cards
Linearization trick: Exponential data (y = Ae^(bx)) becomes linear if you plot ln(y) vs x. Power laws (y = ax^n) linearize as log(y) vs log(x). AP Physics labs use this to identify relationships.
Residuals matter: A good fit has residuals that look random. Patterns in residuals mean your model is wrong — there's structure you haven't captured.
AP connection: AP Physics 1 explicitly requires students to analyze best-fit lines, calculate slopes with units, and interpret y-intercepts physically. R² is tested in AP Statistics.
Why It Matters
Every AP Physics lab report requires a best-fit line and slope interpretation. Understanding R² separates good analysis from guessing.
Machine learning is fundamentally curve fitting at scale — neural networks are regression models with millions of parameters.
Kepler's Third Law (T² ∝ r³) was discovered by noticing that a power law fit planetary data perfectly.
