Concept Explanation

When electric current changes in a primary coil, it creates a changing magnetic flux. This changing flux passes through a neighboring secondary coil and induces an EMF — this is mutual induction.

The strength of coupling is quantified by mutual inductance M. A higher M means even a small dI/dt in the primary creates a large EMF in the secondary. Transformers exploit this principle to step voltage up or down by varying the turns ratio N₂/N₁.

Historical Insight

Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction in 1831. His insight that changing flux — not static fields — produces EMF was the foundation of all modern electrical generators and transformers.

Common Misconception

A constant current does NOT induce EMF. Only a changing current (dI/dt ≠ 0) creates a changing flux and thus an EMF. This is why transformers only work on AC, not DC.
