Concept

A Plinko board is a physical computer that runs the same random experiment thousands of times. Each peg forces the ball to choose left or right — one Bernoulli trial. After n rows of pegs, the position of each ball is the sum of n independent ±1 coin flips.

The binomial distribution counts how many "right" deflections occurred. For large n, the Central Limit Theorem guarantees convergence to a normal (Gaussian) curve — the histogram you see forming in real time.

The bias slider changes p, shifting the mean and skewing the histogram. This is the mechanism behind loaded dice, genetic drift, and A/B test analysis.

Knowledge Cards
Pascal's Triangle: The number of paths to bin k in row n equals C(n,k) — the same value that appears in Pascal's triangle at row n, column k.
Common misconception: "With p=0.5, every ball is equally likely to land in any bin." Wrong — center bins have exponentially more paths than edge bins.
AP connection: Binomial distributions appear on AP Statistics and AP Physics (error analysis). Normal approximation is valid when np ≥ 10 and n(1-p) ≥ 10.
Why It Matters
Galton boards are used in probability education worldwide to demonstrate the Central Limit Theorem physically.
Binomial distributions model radioactive decay counts, genetics (allele inheritance), and clinical trial outcomes.
The normal approximation justifies why measurement errors are Gaussian — a key assumption in AP Physics lab analysis.
