Blackbody Radiation Spectrum / 黑体辐射光谱

Core Formulas
Planck spectral radiance: energy emitted per unit area, per unit solid angle, per unit wavelength
Wien's displacement law: peak wavelength shifts inversely with temperature
Stefan-Boltzmann law: total power radiated per unit area scales as T⁴
Concept

A blackbody is a perfect absorber — and perfect emitter — of electromagnetic radiation. When matter is heated, its charged particles oscillate and radiate. Classical physics predicted infinite energy at short wavelengths (the "ultraviolet catastrophe"). Planck resolved this by hypothesizing that energy is exchanged in discrete quanta: E = hf.

This is the birth of quantum mechanics. Every hot object — a candle, the Sun, a distant star — radiates according to Planck's law. The peak of the spectrum tells us the object's temperature via Wien's law, which is exactly how astronomers measure stellar surface temperatures.

As temperature rises, the peak shifts to shorter (bluer) wavelengths, and total power explodes as T⁴. A star twice as hot emits 16× more power per unit area.

Knowledge Cards

Historical Turning Point

Planck introduced the quantum hypothesis on December 14, 1900 — often called the birthday of quantum physics. He initially considered it a mathematical trick, not physical reality.

Common Misconception

Hotter objects don't just emit "more" of the same light — the entire spectrum shifts. A 3000 K star glows red-orange while a 10000 K star glows blue-white.

Advanced Connection

The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) is a near-perfect blackbody spectrum at T ≈ 2.725 K — the thermal afterglow of the Big Bang, redshifted by cosmic expansion.
