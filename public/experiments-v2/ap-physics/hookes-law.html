Hooke's Law Spring Oscillation · AP Physics
Core Formulas

Restoring force is proportional and opposite to displacement.

Elastic potential energy stored in the spring.

Period depends only on mass and spring constant — not gravity.

Total mechanical energy is conserved (no damping).

Concept

Robert Hooke discovered in 1660 that springs produce a force proportional to how far you stretch or compress them. The negative sign means the force always acts to restore the spring to its natural length — that's why masses oscillate rather than fly away.

The key insight: the period of oscillation is determined entirely by m and k. A stiffer spring (larger k) speeds up the cycle; a heavier mass slows it down. Gravity shifts the equilibrium point but doesn't change the period.

Energy continuously converts between KE and PE. At maximum displacement: all PE, zero KE. At equilibrium: all KE, zero PE. Their sum stays constant — the energy conservation law in action.

Knowledge Cards
HISTORY

Hooke originally published his law as an anagram: "ceiiinosssttuv" — the Latin "ut tensio, sic vis" (as the extension, so the force).

COMMON MISCONCEPTION

Students often think heavier masses oscillate faster. Wrong — larger m means larger inertia, which slows the oscillation (T ∝ √m).

AP CONNECTION

AP Physics 1 Unit 6 (Simple Harmonic Motion): Hooke's Law is the foundation for SHM, energy methods, and the lab practicum.

Why It Matters
  • Car suspension systems use springs tuned to specific k values for ride comfort

  • Building earthquake dampers rely on spring-mass systems to absorb seismic energy
