Concept

Beer's Law states that absorbance A is directly proportional to the concentration c of the absorbing species and the path length l of light through the sample: A = εlc. The molar absorptivity ε (epsilon) is a constant specific to the substance and wavelength.

In a spectrophotometer, a light source emits a specific wavelength, which passes through a cuvette containing the solution. A detector on the other side measures how much light got through (transmittance T = I/I₀). Absorbance A = -log(T) = -log(I/I₀).

A Beer's Law plot (A vs c) is a straight line through the origin. The slope = ε × l. This calibration curve lets you determine unknown concentrations from measured absorbance values — a cornerstone of quantitative analytical chemistry.

Common ε Values
CuSO₄ at 620 nm: ε ≈ 12 L/(mol·cm)
KMnO₄ at 520 nm: ε ≈ 2350 L/(mol·cm)
K₂Cr₂O₇ at 350 nm: ε ≈ 1500 L/(mol·cm)
High ε → very sensitive to small concentrations
Beer's Law Limits
Linear only at low concentrations (typically < 0.01 M). At high c: intermolecular interactions change ε. Stray light, polychromatic light also cause deviations.
AP Chem Connection
Unit 1 & labs: spectroscopy to identify unknowns, determine concentration, follow reaction rates. The A vs c calibration curve is a classic AP lab experiment.
