Concept

Every atom has a nucleus containing protons (positive charge) and neutrons (neutral). The nucleus accounts for >99.9% of atomic mass but only ~10⁻¹⁵ m in radius — 100,000× smaller than the atom itself.

Electrons occupy quantized energy levels (shells) around the nucleus. The Bohr model predicts that hydrogen's electron energies are E_n = −13.6/n² eV, so the ground state (n=1) sits at −13.6 eV, and n=∞ corresponds to ionization.

When electrons drop between levels, they emit photons whose energy equals the level difference. Each element has a unique emission spectrum — the atomic "fingerprint" used in spectroscopy and flame tests.

Balmer Series
Hydrogen transitions to n=2 emit visible light (410–656 nm). H-alpha (656 nm) is the iconic red line. Transitions to n=1 are UV (Lyman series).
Common Misconception
Electrons don't orbit like planets. In quantum mechanics, they exist in probability clouds (orbitals). The Bohr model is a useful approximation for hydrogen only.
AP Chem Connection
Emission spectra confirm quantized energy levels. The photoelectric effect (1905) provided key evidence that light comes in photons of energy E = hν.
