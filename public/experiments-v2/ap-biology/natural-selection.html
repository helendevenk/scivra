Natural Selection
Population Variation · Environmental Pressure · Differential Survival · Trait Frequency · AP Biology
Core Formulas
Selection equation (allele frequency change):
\(\Delta p = \frac{p \cdot q \cdot s}{\bar{W}}\)
p = favored allele freq, q = 1−p, s = selection coefficient, W̄ = mean fitness
Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (no selection):
\(p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1\)
Relative fitness:
\(W = 1 - s \quad (s \in [0,1])\)
Scenario Active
Industrial melanism: dark moths survive better on soot-darkened trees. Before industrialization, light moths dominated. Pollution reversed the selective advantage.
Four Principles of Natural Selection
1. Variation — Individuals in a population differ in heritable traits.
2. Overproduction — More offspring are born than can survive (struggle for existence).
3. Differential survival — Individuals with favorable traits survive and reproduce more.
4. Inheritance — Advantageous traits are passed to offspring, shifting population over generations.
Types of Selection
Directional Selection
One extreme phenotype is favored. Shifts the population mean (e.g., antibiotic resistance, industrial melanism).
Stabilizing Selection
Intermediate phenotypes are favored, extremes eliminated. Reduces variance (e.g., human birth weight).
Disruptive Selection
Both extremes are favored, intermediate eliminated. Can drive speciation (e.g., beak size dimorphism in finches).
Why It Matters
  • Antibiotic resistance evolves in decades — selection coefficient is high in treated patients
  • Mosquito resistance to DDT is classic post-WWII example of rapid directional selection
  • MRSA hospital outbreaks: selection favors methicillin resistance plasmids
  • Balancing selection (heterozygote advantage) maintains sickle cell allele in malaria zones
