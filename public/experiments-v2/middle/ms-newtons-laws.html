Newton's Three Laws
1st Law (Inertia): An object stays at rest or in motion unless acted on by a net force.
2nd Law: Net force equals mass times acceleration.
3rd Law: For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction.
Concept

Newton's three laws explain all everyday motion. The 1st law tells us objects resist change — a hockey puck on ice keeps sliding; a car crash dummy keeps moving when the car stops. The 2nd law quantifies force — pushing a shopping cart harder makes it accelerate faster, and a lighter cart accelerates more for the same push. The 3rd law explains rockets (gas pushed backward, rocket pushed forward) and why you feel a gun's recoil.

Real World Example
Car seat belts apply Newton's 1st law — without them, your body keeps moving forward at the car's speed when the car stops suddenly. Airbags increase the stopping time, reducing the force (F=ma, smaller a = smaller F).
Common Misconception
Heavier objects do NOT fall faster than light ones (in a vacuum). Both experience the same gravitational acceleration g = 9.8 m/s². Air resistance is what makes a feather fall slower than a rock.
Why It Matters (NGSS)
NGSS MS-PS2-1: Apply Newton's 3rd Law to design a solution to a problem. Engineers use F=ma to design safer cars, spacecraft, and roller coasters.
