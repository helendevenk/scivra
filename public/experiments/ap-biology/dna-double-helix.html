Concept Overview

The DNA double helix consists of two antiparallel polynucleotide strands wound around a common axis. The sugar-phosphate backbone runs on the outside, while nitrogenous bases point inward and form complementary pairs via hydrogen bonds.

Antiparallel: One strand runs 5'→3', the other 3'→5'. This is critical for DNA replication and transcription.

Structure Components
Adenine (A) — Purine
Double-ring base. Pairs with Thymine via 2 H-bonds. Weak pair — lower melting temperature.
Thymine (T) — Pyrimidine
Single-ring base. DNA-only (RNA uses Uracil). Pairs with Adenine via 2 H-bonds.
Guanine (G) — Purine
Double-ring base. Pairs with Cytosine via 3 H-bonds. Strong pair — higher melting temperature.
Cytosine (C) — Pyrimidine
Single-ring base. Pairs with Guanine via 3 H-bonds. GC content correlates with thermal stability.
Major & Minor Grooves
The major groove (~22 Å wide) is where most transcription factors and restriction enzymes bind — base sequences are accessible here. The minor groove (~12 Å wide) is narrower and hosts some small molecules like TATA-binding protein.
Why It Matters
  • Complementary base pairing enables faithful DNA replication (semiconservative)
  • GC-rich regions resist strand separation — important in thermophilic organisms
  • Groove geometry allows sequence-specific protein binding without strand opening
  • Anti-cancer drugs (e.g., cisplatin) intercalate between base pairs to halt replication
