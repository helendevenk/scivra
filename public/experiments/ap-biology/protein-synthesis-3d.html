Protein Synthesis Transcription & Translation · AP Biology
Core Formulas
DNA template strand read 3'→5'; mRNA synthesized 5'→3' by RNA polymerase.
Each codon (3 nucleotides) codes for one amino acid; 64 codons, 20 amino acids.
Central Dogma: genetic information flows DNA → RNA → Protein.
Concept

Gene expression has two main stages. Transcription occurs in the nucleus: RNA polymerase binds a promoter, unwinds DNA, and synthesizes a complementary mRNA strand using RNA nucleotides (A, U, G, C — note uracil replaces thymine).

Translation occurs at the ribosome in the cytoplasm. The ribosome reads mRNA codons in the 5'→3' direction. tRNA molecules carrying specific amino acids enter the A site; peptide bonds form at the P site; the growing polypeptide exits from the E site.

The genetic code is universal, redundant (multiple codons per amino acid), and nearly non-overlapping — powerful evidence for the common ancestry of all life.

Key Fact
The ribosome is a ribozyme — the peptidyl transferase activity that forms peptide bonds is catalyzed by rRNA, not protein. This supports the RNA World hypothesis (ribosomes are ancient catalytic RNA machines).
Common Misconception
mRNA does NOT carry the same sequence as the coding strand of DNA — it carries the same sequence as the coding strand (with U instead of T), but is synthesized from the template strand. Students often confuse these.
Why It Matters
AP Biology: protein synthesis is tested in gene expression, mutations, and biotechnology (CRISPR, mRNA vaccines). Understanding how the ribosome reads codons explains how point mutations can change or truncate proteins.
