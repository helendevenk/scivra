Core Formulas

$$1 \text{ diploid (2n)} \xrightarrow{\text{Meiosis}} 4 \text{ haploid (n)}$$

One cell → four genetically unique gametes

$$\text{Genetic variation} = 2^n \times \text{crossing over}$$

n = number of homologous chromosome pairs

$$\text{DNA content: } 4C \to 2C \to 1C$$

DNA halves twice across both meiotic divisions

What is Meiosis?

Meiosis is a specialized cell division that produces four genetically unique haploid cells (gametes) from one diploid parent cell. It consists of two sequential divisions — Meiosis I (reductive) and Meiosis II (equational) — each with four phases: Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, and Telophase.

The key feature distinguishing meiosis from mitosis is crossing over (recombination) during Prophase I, when homologous chromosomes exchange segments at chiasmata. This shuffles alleles and is a major source of genetic diversity.

⚡ Key Distinction

Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes; Meiosis II separates sister chromatids — just like mitosis.

🧬 Common Misconception

Meiosis does NOT always produce 4 identical cells. Crossing over + independent assortment create virtually unlimited genetic combinations.

  • • Sexual reproduction drives evolution via genetic variation
  • • Non-disjunction errors cause Down syndrome (trisomy 21)
