Concept

Animal cells are eukaryotic — they have a true nucleus housing DNA, surrounded by a nuclear envelope with pores. Unlike plant cells, animal cells lack a cell wall and central vacuole, but do contain lysosomes for intracellular digestion.

Organelles divide labor: the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is the manufacturing hub, the Golgi apparatus is the shipping center, and mitochondria are the power plants. Together they form a tightly coordinated endomembrane system.

Membrane-bound compartments allow incompatible chemical reactions to occur simultaneously within the same cell — the key innovation of eukaryotic life.

Historical Fact
Mitochondria were once free-living proteobacteria engulfed ~1.5 billion years ago (endosymbiotic theory, Lynn Margulis, 1967). Evidence: mitochondria have their own circular DNA and ribosomes resembling bacteria.
Common Misconception
The nucleus is NOT the "brain" of the cell — it stores genetic information and coordinates gene expression, but it cannot process sensory input or make decisions the way a brain does. Metaphors mislead!
Why It Matters
AP Biology: understand how organelle dysfunction causes disease (Tay-Sachs = lysosome; Zellweger = peroxisome). Cancer often involves loss of normal cell-cycle checkpoints coordinated in the nucleus.
