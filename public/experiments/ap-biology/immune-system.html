Immune System Adaptive Immunity · AP Biology
Core Concepts
Clonal selection: one B-cell clone produces one specific antibody type.
Antibody structure: 2 heavy + 2 light chains; antigen-binding site = variable region.
Adaptive Immunity Pathways
Humoral (B-cell): APC presents antigen → B-cell activation with T-helper help → clonal expansion → plasma cells secrete antibodies → neutralize antigens.
Cell-Mediated (T-cell): APC presents antigen on MHC-I → cytotoxic T-cell activation → clonal expansion → kill infected cells by inducing apoptosis.
Memory: Both pathways produce long-lived memory cells. Secondary response is 100–1000× faster and stronger.
Clonal Selection Principle
Every person has ~10¹² unique B-cells and T-cells, each with a different receptor. When an antigen enters, only the cells whose receptors fit that antigen are selected, then rapidly proliferate (clonal expansion). This explains immunological specificity and memory.
Common Misconception
Vaccines do NOT train the immune system to be "stronger generally" — they create antigen-specific memory cells. Your immune system's response to the flu is completely independent of its response to tetanus.
Why It Matters
HIV destroys CD4⁺ T-helper cells — collapsing both humoral and cell-mediated responses. Autoimmune diseases (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis) occur when self-tolerance breaks down and immune cells attack host tissue.
