Enzyme Kinetics Michaelis-Menten · AP Biology
Core Formulas
Michaelis-Menten equation: reaction velocity as a function of substrate concentration [S].
Km = substrate concentration at half-maximal velocity. Low Km = high affinity.
Competitive inhibition raises apparent Km; noncompetitive lowers apparent Vmax.
Concept

Enzymes are biological catalysts that lower the activation energy of reactions without being consumed. They bind substrate at the active site to form an enzyme-substrate (ES) complex, then release product and regenerate.

The Michaelis-Menten model describes how reaction rate (v) changes with substrate concentration [S]. At low [S], rate increases linearly; at high [S], active sites are saturated and rate approaches Vmax (zero-order kinetics).

Km is the substrate concentration at which v = ½Vmax — it reflects binding affinity. A small Km means the enzyme has high affinity and reaches half-maximal rate at low substrate concentrations.

Inhibition Mechanisms
Competitive: inhibitor resembles substrate, competes for active site. Km↑, Vmax unchanged. Can be overcome with excess substrate.
Noncompetitive: inhibitor binds allosteric site, changes enzyme shape. Vmax↓, Km unchanged. Cannot be overcome with substrate.
Common Misconception
Enzymes are NOT used up. One enzyme molecule catalyzes thousands of reactions per second (turnover number, kcat). Catalase — one of the fastest enzymes — processes ~40 million H₂O₂ molecules per second.
Why It Matters
AP Biology: enzyme kinetics underlies drug design. Statins competitively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase (cholesterol synthesis). HIV protease inhibitors block viral enzyme. Understanding Km/Vmax helps predict drug dosing and resistance.
