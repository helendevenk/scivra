Key Formulas
Okazaki fragment size (prokaryotes):
\(\text{~1000–2000 nt (prokaryotes)}, \text{~100–200 nt (eukaryotes)}\)
E. coli replication rate:
\(\approx 1000\,\text{bp/s}\)
Error rate (with proofreading):
\(\epsilon \approx 10^{-9} \text{ per bp per replication}\)
Total E. coli genome:
\(4.6 \times 10^6 \text{ bp}, \text{ completed in ~40 min}\)
Enzyme Roles
Helicase
Unwinds the double helix by breaking hydrogen bonds. Moves ahead of the replication fork, using ATP.
SSB Proteins
Single-strand binding proteins stabilize separated template strands, preventing re-annealing.
Primase
Synthesizes short RNA primers (5–10 nt). DNA Pol III cannot initiate de novo — needs a 3'-OH.
DNA Pol III
Main replicase. Adds dNTPs 5'→3'. Also has 3'→5' exonuclease (proofreading). ~1000 bp/s in E. coli.
DNA Ligase
Seals nicks between Okazaki fragments after primers replaced by DNA Pol I. Uses NAD+ (prokaryotes).
Leading vs. Lagging
Leading strand (3'→5' template): synthesized continuously in same direction as fork movement. One primer needed.

Lagging strand (5'→3' template): synthesized discontinuously as Okazaki fragments, each requiring its own primer. Fragments are later joined by ligase after primer removal.
Why It Matters
  • Semiconservative replication (Meselson-Stahl 1958) — each daughter cell gets one original strand
  • Primer requirement explains why DNA can't start fresh — critical for understanding telomere shortening
  • Antibiotic drugs (quinolones) target topoisomerase II (gyrase) to halt replication ahead of helicase
  • Cancer drugs (hydroxyurea) inhibit ribonucleotide reductase, blocking dNTP supply
