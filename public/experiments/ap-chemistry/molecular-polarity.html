Molecular Polarity Dipole Moments & VSEPR Geometries

Core Formulas

Dipole moment: product of charge separation and distance (Debye units)

Net dipole = vector sum of all bond dipoles

Bond angle determines whether dipoles cancel or reinforce

Concept

A molecule is polar if it has a net nonzero dipole moment. Even if individual bonds are polar, the molecular geometry may cause them to cancel. CO₂ has 2 polar C=O bonds, but linear geometry causes the dipoles to point in exactly opposite directions → net dipole = 0 → nonpolar.

H₂O has 2 polar O-H bonds and a bent geometry (104.5°). The dipoles don't cancel — they add to give a net dipole of 1.85 D pointing from the H₂ side toward O. This polarity drives water's extraordinary properties: high boiling point, surface tension, solvent capability.

CHCl₃ (chloroform) is tetrahedral. Three C-Cl bonds pull electron density toward Cl, but the one C-H bond is less polar. The asymmetry gives a net dipole of 1.15 D. BCl₃ is trigonal planar with zero net dipole despite polar B-Cl bonds.

Why Polarity Matters
"Like dissolves like": polar solvents dissolve polar/ionic solutes. Nonpolar molecules have only London dispersion forces. Polar molecules also have dipole-dipole and H-bonding interactions → higher BP.
AP Chem Connection
Unit 3: intermolecular forces, solubility, boiling points. Polarity directly determines IMFs and physical properties.
