Concept

An ideal gas has negligible intermolecular forces and negligible particle volume. The ideal gas law PV = nRT relates pressure, volume, moles, and temperature. At constant n, doubling T doubles P (if V is fixed) — Gay-Lussac's Law.

Pressure arises from particle collisions with walls. More particles, faster particles, or smaller volume → more frequent/forceful collisions → higher pressure. Temperature is proportional to the average kinetic energy: KE_avg = (3/2)kT.

Real gases deviate from ideal behavior at high pressure (particles are close together, intermolecular forces matter) and low temperature (particles slow down, approach condensation). Van der Waals equation adds correction terms 'a' (attraction) and 'b' (volume).

Gas Laws Summary
Boyle's: PV = const (const T,n)
Charles's: V/T = const (const P,n)
Gay-Lussac's: P/T = const (const V,n)
Avogadro's: V/n = const (const T,P)
Maxwell-Boltzmann
Particle speeds follow a distribution. Most probable speed v_p = √(2RT/M). Average speed increases with √T. Heavier gases move slower.
