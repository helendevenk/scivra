Conservation of Momentum · AP Physics 1
Core Formulas
Total momentum is conserved in all collisions (no external forces)
Coefficient of restitution: e=1 elastic, e=0 perfectly inelastic
Post-collision velocities from conservation laws

Concept

Momentum (p = mv) is always conserved when no external forces act. During a collision, internal forces are equal and opposite (Newton's 3rd law), so total momentum stays constant.

Kinetic energy is only conserved in perfectly elastic collisions (e = 1). In real collisions, some KE converts to heat and sound — that's an inelastic collision. "Perfectly inelastic" (e = 0) means the objects stick together.

Watch the momentum data panel: p_total stays constant before and after every collision — that's the law in action.

Key Facts
Historical Context
René Descartes first formulated conservation of momentum (as "quantity of motion") in 1644. Newton later formalized it as a consequence of his 3rd law.
Common Misconception
A heavier ball doesn't always "win" a collision. What matters is momentum (mv), not mass alone. A fast light ball can have more momentum than a slow heavy one.
AP Connection
AP Physics 1 Big Idea 4: Interactions can be described using the concept of force or in terms of momentum. Impulse-momentum theorem: J = F·Δt = Δp.
Why It Matters
▸Rocket propulsion uses conservation of momentum: expelled gas going backward pushes rocket forward
▸Particle physics detectors use momentum conservation to identify collision products
▸Airbags increase collision time (Δt) to reduce peak force: F = Δp/Δt
