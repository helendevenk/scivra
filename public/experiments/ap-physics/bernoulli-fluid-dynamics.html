Concept

Bernoulli's equation is essentially energy conservation for a flowing fluid. It tells us that if a fluid speeds up (kinetic energy increases), its pressure must drop — there's a constant "budget" of energy per unit volume.

The Venturi effect demonstrates this perfectly: squeeze a pipe narrower and the fluid accelerates (continuity equation). That faster fluid has lower pressure, which is why a manometer at the throat shows a pressure drop.

Airplane wings work on this principle: the curved upper surface forces air to travel faster, creating lower pressure above the wing and producing lift.

Knowledge Cards

Historical InsightDaniel Bernoulli published his equation in 1738. It assumes steady, incompressible, inviscid (frictionless) flow — a useful idealization for many real-world scenarios.
Common MisconceptionBernoulli's principle does NOT fully explain airplane lift — wing angle of attack and air viscosity also matter. But it contributes a significant portion.
AP ConnectionAP Physics B: Bernoulli's equation, continuity, and venturi tubes are explicitly tested. Know both the equation and its assumptions.

Why It Matters

• Carburetors use the Venturi effect to mix fuel with air in internal combustion engines.
• Medical nebulizers atomize medicine using low-pressure zones in fast-moving airstreams.
• Pitot tubes on aircraft measure airspeed by comparing static and dynamic pressure.
