Concept

Matter exists in different states depending on how much thermal energy its molecules have relative to the intermolecular forces holding them together. In a solid, molecules vibrate about fixed positions — strong bonds win. In a liquid, molecules slide past each other but stay close. In a gas, molecules fly freely.

Phase transitions happen at specific temperatures (at a given pressure) because intermolecular potential energy must be overcome without changing kinetic energy — that is why temperature stays flat during melting and boiling even as you keep adding heat. That energy goes into breaking bonds, not speeding up molecules.

The phase diagram maps which state is stable at each (T, P) combination. The triple point is where all three phases coexist. Beyond the critical point, gas and liquid become indistinguishable — that is a supercritical fluid.

Knowledge Cards

Why does ice float?

Water is anomalous: its solid phase (ice) is less dense than liquid water. The hydrogen-bond network in ice creates an open hexagonal structure. This is why the solid-liquid boundary in water's phase diagram slopes slightly backward (to the left), unlike most substances.

Plasma: The 4th State

At extremely high temperatures (stars, lightning, fusion reactors), electrons are stripped from atoms, creating ionized plasma — the most common state of matter in the visible universe. Plasma responds strongly to electromagnetic fields.

AP Exam Tip

Phase diagrams appear regularly on AP Physics 2 and AP Chemistry exams. Know: (1) melting point increases with pressure for most substances, (2) boiling point increases with pressure, (3) triple point is unique, (4) latent heat = flat region on heating curve.

Why It Matters
Refrigerators and air conditioners exploit phase transitions: refrigerant absorbs heat when it evaporates, releases heat when it condenses.
Industrial processes like steel casting, semiconductor crystal growth, and food preservation all require precise control of phase transitions.
Climate science depends on water's unusual phase properties — melting ice caps, cloud formation, and ocean heat capacity all hinge on H₂O phase behavior.
