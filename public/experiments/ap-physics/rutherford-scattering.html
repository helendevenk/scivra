Concept

In 1909, Geiger and Marsden fired alpha particles at gold foil and found that roughly 1 in 8000 bounced back at large angles. This was "as if you fired artillery shells at tissue paper and they came back at you." The Thomson plum-pudding model predicted only tiny deflections.

Rutherford's nuclear model explained it: the positive charge is concentrated in a tiny, dense nucleus. Alpha particles passing far from the nucleus (large impact parameter b) are barely deflected. Those aimed nearly head-on experience intense Coulomb repulsion and scatter at large angles.

The hyperbolic trajectories are governed entirely by Coulomb repulsion. The simulation uses Velocity Verlet integration to compute each trajectory numerically from Newton's law and Coulomb's force.

Knowledge Cards

The Experiment

Gold was chosen for its malleability (foil ~1000 atoms thick) and high Z (79). Alpha particles from Ra-226 had ~4.8 MeV kinetic energy. Detectors recorded flashes on a zinc sulfide screen.

What It Proved

The nucleus is ~10⁻¹⁵ m (1 fm) — about 10,000× smaller than the atom (~10⁻¹⁰ m). Atoms are mostly empty space. This replaced Thomson's model permanently.

Advanced Connection

Rutherford's formula N(θ) ∝ 1/sin⁴(θ/2) predicts the angular distribution of scattered particles — and it matches experiment perfectly for long-range Coulomb scattering.
