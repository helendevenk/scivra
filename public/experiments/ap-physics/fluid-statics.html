Core Formulas

Hydrostatic pressure increases linearly with depth h

Condition for floating — object density ≤ fluid density

Pressure Gradient in Fluids

In a static fluid, pressure increases with depth because every layer of fluid must support the weight of all fluid above it. The pressure gradient is dP/dh = ρg — constant for an incompressible fluid.

The color gradient in the 3D container visualizes this: lighter blue near the surface where pressure is lowest, darkening to deep blue at the bottom where ρgh is maximum.

Archimedes discovered that any submerged object displaces fluid equal to its own volume. The fluid "pushes back" with the weight of that displaced volume — this is buoyancy.

Key Facts

Isobaric Surfaces

In a static fluid, all points at the same depth are at the same pressure — regardless of horizontal position. This is why containers of different shapes have equal pressure at equal depth.

Common Misconception

Buoyancy doesn't depend on weight — it depends on volume displaced. A hollow steel ship floats because it displaces more water volume than a solid steel block of the same mass.

AP Connection

Why It Matters

  • Ship design: steel hull displaces enough water to provide buoyancy for the entire vessel
  • Submarines control depth by adjusting ballast water (changing average density)
  • Dam engineering: wall thickness increases toward base due to larger ρgh forces
