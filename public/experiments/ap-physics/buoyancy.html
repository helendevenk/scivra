Buoyancy (Advanced)

Archimedes' Principle · AP Physics

Core Formulas

Buoyant force equals the weight of fluid displaced
Object floats when net force ≥ 0 (buoyancy ≥ weight)
Fraction submerged equals density ratio

Concept

Archimedes discovered that any object placed in a fluid experiences an upward force equal to the weight of fluid it displaces. This buoyant force arises from the pressure difference between the bottom and top of the submerged object — deeper fluid exerts more pressure, creating a net upward push.

Whether an object sinks or floats depends entirely on the comparison of its density to the fluid. If ρobj < ρfluid, the object floats with only part of its volume submerged — exactly enough to displace fluid equal to its own weight.

Submarines exploit this by adjusting ballast tanks: filling them with water increases average density to sink; expelling water decreases it to rise.

Did You Know?

Historical InsightArchimedes (~250 BC) reportedly leaped from his bath shouting "Eureka!" upon realizing displaced water volume equals the submerged object's volume.
Common MisconceptionBuoyancy doesn't depend on the object's weight directly — only on the volume of fluid displaced and that fluid's density.
AP ConnectionAP Physics B/C: buoyancy, pressure-depth relationship, and fluid statics are tested topics. Fraction submerged = ρ_obj / ρ_fluid is a key result.

Why It Matters

• Ships are made of steel but float because their hollow hull displaces vast volumes of water.
• Hot air balloons float because heated air is less dense than cool surrounding air.
• Medical centrifuges separate blood components by exploiting density differences in fluid.
