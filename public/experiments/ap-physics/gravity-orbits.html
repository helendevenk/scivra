Gravity and Orbits

60 FPS
Universal Gravitation
Every mass attracts every other mass with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
Orbital Velocity
The speed needed for a circular orbit at radius r. Objects moving faster will enter elliptical or escape trajectories.
Kepler's Third Law
The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis. This holds for all elliptical orbits around the same central mass.
Kepler's Laws Explained
1st Law: Law of Ellipses
Planets move in elliptical orbits with the star at one focus. A circle is a special case with eccentricity = 0.
2nd Law: Equal Areas
A line from the planet to the star sweeps equal areas in equal time intervals. Planets move faster near perihelion.
3rd Law: Harmonic Law
T^2 / r^3 = constant for all orbits around the same star. Farther planets have longer periods.

Q1: If you double the orbital radius, how does the orbital period change?
From Kepler's 3rd law: T^2 ~ r^3, so T ~ r^(3/2). If r doubles, T increases by 2^(3/2) = 2*sqrt(2) ≈ 2.83.
Q2: What happens to gravitational force if both the star mass and distance are doubled?
F = GMm/r^2. Double M: F*2. Double r: F/4. Net: 2/4 = 1/2. The force is halved.
