Ideal Gas Law & Thermodynamics — AP Physics

Core Formulas
Ideal Gas Law: P=pressure (Pa), V=volume (m³), n=moles, R=8.314 J/mol·K, T=temperature (K)
Average translational kinetic energy per molecule — depends only on temperature, not molecular mass
Root-mean-square speed of molecules; heavier molecules move slower at the same temperature
Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution: probability density of molecular speeds at temperature T
Concept

The ideal gas law PV = nRT connects four macroscopic state variables. "Ideal" means we assume molecules have no volume and no intermolecular forces — a great approximation at low pressure and high temperature.

What you're really watching in the 3D box is the microscopic picture: molecules bouncing off walls. Pressure is the macroscopic average of billions of momentum transfers per second. Temperature is the average kinetic energy per molecule — not a single molecule's property.

The Maxwell-Boltzmann speed distribution shows that molecules in a gas have a wide range of speeds. At higher T, the entire distribution shifts right and flattens — more molecules are moving faster on average.

Knowledge Cards

Why Kelvin, not Celsius?

At 0 K (absolute zero), all molecular motion stops. PV = nRT requires an absolute scale. Celsius is just a shifted version of Kelvin — using it would give negative pressures at low temperatures, which is nonsense.

Real Gas vs Ideal Gas

At very high pressures or low temperatures, real gases deviate from PV=nRT because molecules do have volume (excluded volume) and attract each other (van der Waals forces). The van der Waals equation corrects for both.

AP Exam Insight

For AP Physics, memorize R = 8.314 J/mol·K and k_B = 1.38×10⁻²³ J/K. Note that nR = Nk_B where N is total molecules. Both forms appear on AP problems.

Why It Matters
Weather balloons, scuba tanks, car tires — all governed by ideal gas relationships.
Internal combustion engines rely on the pressure spike when combustion quickly heats gas inside a fixed-ish volume.
Semiconductor manufacturing uses ultra-pure gases at precise partial pressures — ideal gas law is the engineering foundation.
