Concept

Moment of inertia is the rotational analog of mass — it measures how hard it is to change an object's spin. Unlike mass, it depends on how mass is distributed relative to the rotation axis.

A ring has more inertia than a disk of the same mass and radius because all its mass is far from the center. Apply the same torque and the disk accelerates faster: α = τ/I.

Switch between Disk, Ring, and Sphere above. Watch how the same torque produces different angular accelerations — the relationship τ = Iα is the rotational Newton's 2nd Law.

Key Insights
Mass Distribution Matters
A figure skater pulls arms in to reduce I, increasing ω (angular momentum L = Iω = const). Same principle: less I → faster spin for same L.
Disk vs. Ring Race
If a disk and ring roll down a ramp from the same height, the disk always wins — smaller I means more KE goes to translation, less to rotation.
AP Connection
AP Physics 1 Unit 7: Torque, rotational kinematics, and conservation of angular momentum. Rotational analogs of linear equations are frequently tested.
Why It Matters

• Flywheels in electric vehicles store energy as rotational KE, then release it for acceleration bursts.

• Gyroscopes in phones, drones, and spacecraft use conservation of angular momentum for orientation control.

• Engineers choose wheel geometry (disk vs. ring) to optimize vehicle performance and handling.
