Core Formulas
Horizontal position — constant velocity (no air resistance)
Vertical position — free fall from initial height h₀
Range on flat ground (h₀ = 0) — maximum at θ = 45°
Maximum height — occurs when vertical velocity = 0
How It Works

Projectile motion is the superposition of two completely independent motions: constant horizontal velocity (vₓ = v₀cosθ, no force acts horizontally) and uniformly accelerated vertical motion (ay = –g, gravity only acts vertically).

This simulation uses Velocity Verlet integration with adaptive sub-stepping. The parabolic trajectory is drawn in real time as a BufferGeometry line, and a glowing ring marks the landing point. No air resistance — in reality, drag significantly reduces range at high speeds.

Try complementary angles (30° and 60°): they give the same range but reach different heights and have different flight times. Maximum range occurs at exactly 45°.

Knowledge Cards

Symmetry of Trajectories

Angles adding to 90° (like 30°+60°, 20°+70°) give the same horizontal range from flat ground. This follows from R = v₀²sin(2θ)/g and the identity sin(2θ) = sin(180°−2θ).

Common Misconception

A horizontally launched ball and one dropped from the same height hit the ground simultaneously. Both start with zero vertical velocity and experience the same g — horizontal speed is irrelevant to vertical fall time.

AP Connection

AP Physics 1 tests: independence of x/y motions, time-of-flight derivations (solving quadratic for h₀ ≠ 0), and range calculations. Launched-from-height problems require the full quadratic — practice with the Height slider.
