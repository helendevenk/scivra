How It Works

A simple pendulum is a mass on a string that swings under gravity. The restoring force is the tangential component of gravity: F = –mg sinθ. For small angles, sinθ ≈ θ (radians), giving simple harmonic motion with period T = 2π√(L/g).

This simulation solves the exact nonlinear equation α = –(g/L)sinθ using Velocity Verlet integration. At large angles (try 80°!), the measured period is noticeably longer than the small-angle formula predicts — that's the nonlinear correction at work.

Notice that the period is independent of mass and (for small angles) amplitude. Only length and gravitational acceleration matter.

Knowledge Cards

Historical Insight

Galileo reportedly observed pendulum isochronism using his own pulse as a timer while watching a chandelier swing in the Pisa cathedral (~1583). Huygens later used pendulums to build precise clocks in 1656.

Common Misconception

Many students assume heavier bobs swing faster. In reality, mass cancels out of the equation of motion — a 1 kg and a 10 kg bob on the same string have identical periods.

AP Connection

AP Physics 1 explicitly tests period vs. length graphs (T² ∝ L) and the breakdown of small-angle approximation. Large-angle behavior is a standard free-response topic.

Why It Matters
Mechanical clocks — pendulum period determines timekeeping precision
Seismometers use pendulum principles to detect ground motion
Foucault pendulum demonstrates Earth's rotation at science museums worldwide
AP Physics 1 / SAT II: period-length relationship and energy conservation are recurring exam topics
