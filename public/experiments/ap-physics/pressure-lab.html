Force on a surface area due to pressure

How Pressure Works

Pressure in a fluid increases with depth because deeper layers support the weight of all fluid above them. This is described by P = ρgh, where every meter deeper adds ρg pascals of pressure.

A U-tube manometer works by balancing fluid columns. The height difference between the two arms directly shows pressure difference — taller fluid column on one side means higher pressure pushing on the other.

Pascal's Principle tells us pressure applied to an enclosed fluid transmits undiminished throughout. This powers hydraulic brakes, elevators, and presses.

Key Facts

Mercury Manometer

Mercury (ρ = 13,600 kg/m³) is used because it produces measurable column heights for atmospheric pressure — ~760 mm Hg = 101,325 Pa.

Common Misconception

Pressure depends only on depth and density, NOT on the total volume of fluid. A narrow tube and a wide tank at the same depth have the same pressure.

AP Connection

AP Physics 2 expects you to calculate gauge pressure, use Pascal's Principle for hydraulics, and interpret manometer readings.

Why It Matters

  • Hydraulic car lifts multiply force using Pascal's Principle
  • Scuba depth limits set by pressure increase with depth
  • Blood pressure measured in mmHg uses mercury column principle
