Capacitors & RC Circuits V(t) = V₀(1−e^(−t/τ)) · AP Physics
Core Formulas
Capacitor charges toward V₀ with time constant τ = RC.
Current decays exponentially from V₀/R as capacitor fills.
Energy stored in the electric field between the plates.
Concept

A capacitor stores charge on two conducting plates separated by an insulator. When connected to a resistor and voltage source, charge builds up exponentially — slow at first because the voltage difference is large, then progressively slower as the capacitor "fills up." At t = τ = RC, the voltage reaches 63.2% of V₀.

Discharging is the mirror process: V(t) = V₀·e^(−t/RC). After 5τ, less than 1% of the initial charge remains. This time-scaling property is the basis of camera flashes, timing circuits, and power supply filters.

Historical Fact
The Leyden jar (1745) was the first capacitor — a glass jar with metal foil inside and outside. It could store enough charge to knock a person down.
Common Misconception
The capacitor is "fully charged" after τ — FALSE. At t=τ, only 63.2% is charged. "Full" charge is approached asymptotically; 99% requires t ≈ 4.6τ.
Why It Matters (AP Physics)
