Core Formulas

Series: softer than either spring alone. Same force, displacement adds.

Parallel: stiffer than either spring alone. Same displacement, force adds.

Period uses the effective spring constant k_eff.

Total energy of the coupled two-spring system.

Concept

Series: Connect two springs end-to-end. The same force pulls on each spring, but their extensions add together. The effective constant obeys 1/k_eff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂ — always smaller than either alone. Think of it as the weakest link in a chain.

Parallel: Connect two springs side-by-side carrying the same load. The same displacement stretches both, but their restoring forces add. k_eff = k₁ + k₂ — always larger. Two hands are stronger than one.

Use the Mode toggle to switch between configurations. Watch how k_eff changes — and how the Period responds. For series, equal springs give k_eff = k/2; for parallel, k_eff = 2k.

Knowledge Cards
ANALOGY

Springs in series behave like resistors in parallel (both give a smaller combined value). Springs in parallel behave like resistors in series. This confuses students — always derive from the physics, don't memorize the analogy!

IMPORTANT CONSTRAINT

Series formula assumes springs are massless and the connection point is massless too. If there is an intermediate mass, you have a coupled oscillator — a much harder problem!

AP CONNECTION

AP Physics C: Mechanics includes multi-spring problems. Common exam question: "Find the effective spring constant and period when springs are connected in series vs. parallel."

Why It Matters
  • Vehicle suspension systems combine springs in complex parallel/series arrangements to tune ride quality independently from load capacity
  • Atomic bond networks in crystals can be modeled as spring lattices — series/parallel rules predict sound velocity
  • MEMS devices tune resonant frequency by switching spring configurations electronically
