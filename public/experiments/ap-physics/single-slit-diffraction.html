Concept

When light passes through a slit comparable in width to its wavelength, it spreads out—this is diffraction. Each infinitesimal point in the slit acts as its own wave source (Huygens-Fresnel principle), and these wavelets interfere with each other on the screen.

The result is a bright central maximum flanked by weaker secondary maxima, with dark minima (zeros) at precise angles. Narrowing the slit makes the central peak wider—the classic wave-optics surprise.

Key Insights
Common Misconception: Narrower slit → narrower pattern. Wrong! Smaller a → larger α → wider central max.
Historical Note: Augustin-Jean Fresnel unified Huygens' construction with Young's interference in 1818, founding wave optics.
AP Connection: Single-slit minima at a·sinθ = mλ (m≠0); double-slit fringes occur between them.
Why It Matters
  • X-ray crystallography uses diffraction to determine atomic structure
  • Optical instruments: diffraction sets the resolution limit
  • Radio telescopes: slit-like aperture diffraction governs angular resolution
