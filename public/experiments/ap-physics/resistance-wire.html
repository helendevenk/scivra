Resistance of a Wire R = ρL/A · AP Physics
Core Formulas
Resistance depends on material resistivity ρ, length L, and cross-sectional area A.
Temperature changes resistivity: α is the temperature coefficient.
Ohm's Law: Drift speed v_d relates to current density J and carrier density n.
Concept

Resistance is a material's opposition to current flow. Doubling the length doubles resistance — electrons collide more often. Doubling the cross-section halves resistance — more paths available in parallel. Materials differ in resistivity by orders of magnitude: copper (~1.7×10⁻⁸ Ω·m) vs nichrome (~1.1×10⁻⁶ Ω·m), a 64× difference.

Temperature matters: in metals, higher temperature → more lattice vibrations → more scattering → higher resistance. This is why nichrome heating elements glow — their high resistivity concentrates power dissipation.

Historical Fact
Georg Ohm (1827) published his law when most physicists believed electricity was too mysterious to quantify. He was dismissed; vindication came 16 years later via the Royal Society Copley Medal.
Common Misconception
Electrons don't actually move fast — drift speed in copper at 1A/mm² is only ~0.1 mm/s. The electric field propagates at ~c, not the electrons.
