Nuclear Decay & Radioactivity / 核衰变与放射性

Core Formulas
Radioactive decay law: N₀ is initial count, λ is decay constant (s⁻¹)
Half-life: time for half of nuclei to decay; λ = ln2 / T½
Activity A (decays/second) = Becquerels (Bq)
Concept

Radioactive decay is a quantum mechanical process — each unstable nucleus has a fixed probability per unit time of decaying, regardless of its history or environment. This leads to a pure exponential decrease in the number of remaining nuclei.

Alpha decay ejects a helium-4 nucleus (2p + 2n), reducing A by 4 and Z by 2. Beta-minus decay converts a neutron to a proton, emitting an electron and antineutrino. Gamma emission releases high-energy photons without changing A or Z.

The half-life T½ is the key observable: it is invariant and independent of sample size. Carbon-14 dating uses T½ = 5730 years to determine the age of organic materials up to ~50,000 years old.

Knowledge Cards

Why Exponential?

Because each nucleus decays independently with constant probability per unit time: dN/dt = −λN. This first-order ODE has the unique solution N(t) = N₀e^(−λt).

Common Misconception

After one half-life, exactly half have decayed — but this is only true statistically for large N. For small samples, individual decay events are truly random.

Real-World Scale

1 Curie (Ci) = 3.7×10¹⁰ decays/s (activity of 1g Ra-226). 1 Becquerel = 1 decay/s. Nuclear power plants handle TBq to PBq activities.
