Concept

Light slows down when entering a denser medium. Since frequency is fixed (determined by the source), the wavelength shrinks, and the wavefronts bunch together. This change in wave speed is what bends the ray — the direction must shift so the wavefront stays coherent across the interface.

When light tries to exit a denser medium at too steep an angle, there is no real solution for a transmitted angle — total internal reflection (TIR) occurs. Every photon bounces back. Fiber optics, prism binoculars, and diamonds exploit this.

Key Insights
Common Misconception: Light "chooses" to bend. It doesn't — the wave phase must be continuous across the interface, forcing the direction change.
Historical Note: Ibn al-Haytham first described refraction quantitatively (~1000 CE); Willebrord Snellius derived the sine law in 1621.
AP Connection: TIR only occurs from denser to less dense (n₁ > n₂). Critical angle θ_c = arcsin(n₂/n₁).
Why It Matters
  • Fiber optic cables: TIR guides signals around the globe
  • Camera lenses: multiple refracting surfaces correct aberrations
  • Mirages: atmospheric refraction bends light near hot ground
  • Diamond brilliance: high n = 2.42 creates TIR sparkle
