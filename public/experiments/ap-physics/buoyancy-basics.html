Concept

When an object floats, two forces are perfectly balanced: its weight pulling down and the buoyant force pushing up. The buoyant force equals the weight of water that the object pushes out of the way (displaces).

Add mass to a boat, and it sinks lower — displacing more water until the new, larger buoyant force matches the heavier weight. If you add too much mass, the boat can't displace enough water before it runs out of hull, and it sinks!

The stability margin tells you how much extra mass the hull can accept before flooding.

Knowledge Cards

Real LifeA cargo ship can carry thousands of tons because its steel hull is hollow — the average density of ship + air inside is less than water.
Common MistakeStudents often think heavier objects always sink. But a steel battleship floats while a solid steel ball sinks — shape and hollow volume matter!
Why It MattersEngineers must calculate exactly how low a ship will sit when fully loaded to ensure water doesn't pour over the deck (freeboard calculation).

Why It Matters

• Ship designers use load line marks ("Plimsoll line") to ensure vessels aren't overloaded.
• Pontoon bridges use buoyancy to support vehicle weight over water.
• Life vests keep people afloat by adding low-density foam volume.
