Diffusion & Brownian Motion Random Walk · Mean Squared Displacement

Core Formulas

Stokes-Einstein relation: D increases with temperature, decreases with particle size

Mean squared displacement grows linearly with time (3D diffusion)

Fick's first law: flux is proportional to concentration gradient

Concept

Brownian motion is the seemingly random jiggling of particles suspended in a fluid, caused by constant collisions with smaller fluid molecules. Einstein (1905) showed this is the same physical process that drives net diffusion from high to low concentration.

Higher temperature → more energetic fluid molecules → harder random kicks → faster diffusion. Larger particles → more drag from fluid → slower diffusion. This is captured by the Stokes-Einstein equation D = kT/(6πηr).

On average, a particle travels nowhere — but the mean squared displacement (average of r² over many particles) grows linearly with time. This ⟨r²⟩ = 6Dt is the statistical fingerprint of diffusion in 3D.

🔬 Historical Fact
Einstein's 1905 paper on Brownian motion gave indirect proof of atoms — before most physicists accepted atomic theory.
⚠️ Common Misconception
Diffusion doesn't mean every particle moves from high to low concentration. Individual particles move randomly — the net flux is from statistics.
🔗 AP Connection

Why It Matters

  • • Drug delivery: nanoparticle diffusion through tissue determines therapeutic reach
  • • Cell biology: nutrients and signaling molecules spread by diffusion, not active transport
  • • Finance: the Black-Scholes model for stock prices is mathematically identical to diffusion
