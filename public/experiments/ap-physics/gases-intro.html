Gases: Introduction AP Physics / Thermodynamics

Core Formulas

Ideal Gas Law — relates pressure, volume, moles, temperature

Boyle's Law — at constant T, pressure and volume are inversely proportional

Charles' Law — at constant P, volume is proportional to temperature

Gay-Lussac's Law — at constant V, pressure is proportional to temperature

How Gases Work

Gas molecules are in constant, random motion. They collide with each other and with container walls, creating pressure. The faster they move (higher temperature), the harder and more frequently they hit — raising pressure.

Reduce the container size and molecules hit the walls more often per second, increasing pressure. This is Boyle's Law in action: smaller volume → higher pressure at fixed temperature.

The ideal gas approximation assumes molecules have negligible volume and no intermolecular forces — a very good model at low pressures and high temperatures.

Key Facts

Universal Gas Constant

R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) — same for all ideal gases everywhere in the universe.

Common Misconception

Temperature measures average kinetic energy per molecule — not total energy. More molecules = more total energy even at same T.

AP Connection

PV = nRT is central to AP Physics 2. Expect to calculate P, V, or T after one variable changes while others are held fixed.

Why It Matters

  • Car engines compress air-fuel mix — Boyle's Law determines compression ratio
  • Hot-air balloons use Charles' Law: heat air → expand → become buoyant
  • Pressure cookers raise boiling point via Gay-Lussac's Law
