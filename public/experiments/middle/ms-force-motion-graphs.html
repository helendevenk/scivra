Force & Motion Graphs
Learning Objectives
• Apply Newton's second law: F_net = ma to calculate acceleration
• Use kinematic equations to predict position and velocity from initial conditions
• Interpret position-time, velocity-time, and acceleration-time graphs
• Explain how friction and air resistance affect net force and motion
• Identify constant velocity, constant acceleration, and deceleration on graphs
Key Formulas
Newton's Second Law
F = ma — Net force equals the product of mass and acceleration. When the net force is zero, velocity is constant (Newton's first law — inertia). Only a non-zero net force causes acceleration.
The 3D car simulation integrates Newton's law in real time: acceleration = F_net / mass, velocity += a × dt, position += v × dt. When velocity reaches ±10 m/s, the car bounces back to simulate a finite track.
Reading the Graphs
Position (green) — the slope of the position-time graph equals velocity. Curved = accelerating; straight = constant velocity.
Velocity (blue) — the slope equals acceleration. Horizontal line = zero acceleration; rising line = positive acceleration.
Acceleration (yellow) — horizontal line on the a-t graph indicates constant acceleration. Area under a-t graph = change in velocity.
Real-World Applications
Vehicle Crash Testing Roller Coaster Design Projectile Motion Sports Biomechanics Rocket Trajectory Traffic Engineering
Traffic engineers use position-time and velocity-time analysis to design safe following distances and braking zones. Understanding the area under a velocity-time graph (= displacement) is critical for highway design.
