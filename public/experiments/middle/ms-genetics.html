Genetics & DNA
Learning Objectives

Key Formulas
DNA Double Helix
DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is organized as a double helix — two complementary strands wound around a common axis. The strands are antiparallel and held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary nitrogenous bases.
Base pairing rules (Chargaff):
• Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T) via 2 hydrogen bonds
• Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C) via 3 hydrogen bonds
These specific pairings allow faithful DNA replication during cell division, preserving genetic information across generations with very high accuracy.
Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium
In an idealized population (large size, random mating, no selection/mutation/migration), allele frequencies remain constant from generation to generation. This is the Hardy-Weinberg principle, used as a null hypothesis in population genetics.
If p = frequency of dominant allele A and q = frequency of recessive allele a, then p + q = 1 and the genotype frequencies are p² (AA), 2pq (Aa), and q² (aa).
Real populations deviate because mutations, selection, genetic drift, gene flow, and non-random mating continuously act on gene pools. The simulation here models how mutation pressure alone shifts allele frequencies.
Gene Loci & Mutations
A gene locus is a fixed position on a chromosome where a particular gene is located. The glowing gold markers on the helix represent four gene loci. Alleles are alternative forms of a gene — dominant alleles mask the expression of recessive alleles in heterozygotes.
Mutations are random changes to the DNA sequence. Point mutations change a single nucleotide; chromosomal mutations rearrange larger segments. The mutation rate slider controls how quickly p shifts each generation.
Real-World Applications
Genetic Disease Screening Forensic DNA Profiling CRISPR Gene Editing Cancer Research Evolutionary Biology Agriculture GMOs
Hardy-Weinberg calculations are used by epidemiologists to estimate carrier frequencies for recessive genetic diseases like cystic fibrosis (q ≈ 0.02) or sickle cell anemia in specific populations, guiding screening programs.
