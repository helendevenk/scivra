Photosynthesis & Respiration
Learning Objectives
• Write balanced equations for photosynthesis and cellular respiration
• Identify the reactants and products of each process
• Explain the role of chloroplasts in converting light energy to chemical energy
• Describe how mitochondria produce ATP through aerobic respiration
• Compare and contrast the two processes as opposites on the carbon cycle
Key Equations
Photosynthesis
Photosynthesis occurs in the chloroplasts (green organelle, left side). Light energy drives the conversion of CO₂ and H₂O into glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) and oxygen. The light-dependent reactions occur in thylakoid membranes; the Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma.
The light intensity slider controls how fast photosynthesis runs — more light means more ATP and NADPH from light reactions, speeding up the Calvin cycle and glucose production.
Cellular Respiration
Cellular respiration occurs in the mitochondria (red organelle, right side). Glucose is broken down in 3 stages: glycolysis (cytoplasm), Krebs cycle (matrix), and the electron transport chain (inner membrane). Net yield: ~36-38 ATP per glucose.
The CO₂ concentration slider affects photosynthesis rate directly. At high CO₂, more glucose is made; at low CO₂, photosynthesis is carbon-limited regardless of light availability.
Energy Flow
The two processes are coupled in ecosystems: photosynthesis stores solar energy in chemical bonds (glucose), and respiration releases that energy as ATP for cellular work. Together they drive the global carbon and oxygen cycles.
Real-World Applications

Understanding the CO₂-dependence of photosynthesis is critical for modeling how rising atmospheric CO₂ affects plant growth and global food security under climate change scenarios.
