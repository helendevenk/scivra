Plate Tectonics
Learning Objectives
• Identify the three types of plate boundaries: divergent, convergent, and transform
• Explain how convection currents in the mantle drive plate motion
• Describe how subduction zones form ocean trenches and volcanic arcs
• Relate plate boundary type to specific geological features and hazards
• Quantify plate motion in cm/year and relate to geological timescales
Key Concepts
Boundary Types
Divergent boundary — plates move apart. Magma upwells to fill the gap, forming new oceanic crust at mid-ocean ridges. Example: Mid-Atlantic Ridge (spreading at ~2.5 cm/yr).
Convergent boundary (subduction) — denser oceanic plate dives beneath continental plate. Creates deep ocean trenches, volcanic mountains, and earthquake zones. Example: Cascadia, Japan Trench.
Transform boundary — plates slide horizontally past each other. No crust is created or destroyed, but friction generates powerful shallow-focus earthquakes. Example: San Andreas Fault.
Convection in the Mantle
Heat from Earth's core drives slow convection currents in the semi-solid mantle. Hot mantle material rises near ridges, spreads laterally, cools, and sinks at subduction zones. This "conveyor belt" drags the lithospheric plates above it at speeds of 1–20 cm per year.
The convection speed slider controls how fast the simulated mantle moves, which directly drives plate separation rate. Density contrast controls which plate subducts at convergent margins.
Real-World Applications
Earthquake Prediction Volcano Monitoring Tsunami Warning Mountain Building Mineral Deposits Seafloor Spreading
Plate tectonic models help geologists predict where future large earthquakes are most likely (seismic gaps), inform building codes, and locate hydrothermal vent mineral deposits valuable for mining.
