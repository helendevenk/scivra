Atmospheric Layers
Troposphere 0–12 km · Contains 75% of atmospheric mass and virtually all weather. Temperature decreases from ~15°C at surface to ~−60°C at tropopause. Lapse rate ~6.5°C/km.
Stratosphere 12–50 km · Contains the ozone layer (O₃) which absorbs UV radiation. Temperature increases with altitude from −60°C to ~0°C at stratopause. Aircraft cruise here (less turbulence).
Mesosphere 50–80 km · Coldest layer, −100°C at mesopause. Meteors (shooting stars) burn up here by friction. Noctilucent clouds form here at high latitudes.
Thermosphere 80–700 km · Temperature rises dramatically to 1,500°C (though gas is very thin). ISS orbits here (~400 km). Aurora borealis/australis occur here as solar particles excite atmospheric gases.
Exosphere 700 km+ · Outermost layer where gas molecules escape to space. No distinct upper boundary — gradually fades into interplanetary medium. Hydrogen and helium dominate.
Temperature Profile

Temperature in the atmosphere does not decrease monotonically with altitude. The stratosphere warms because ozone (O₃) absorbs UV. The thermosphere warms because extreme UV dissociates molecules and X-rays ionize the thin gas. The mesosphere cools because there is too little ozone above (too little UV heating) and insufficient greenhouse gas below. This layered temperature structure creates stable layers separated by "pauses" (tropopause, stratopause, mesopause).

Key Equations
Hydrostatic equation:
\(\frac{dP}{dz} = -\rho g\)
Pressure decreases with altitude at rate proportional to density. ~50% of atmosphere is below 5.5 km altitude.
Barometric formula:
\(P(z) = P_0 e^{-z/H}\)
H = scale height ≈ 8.5 km for Earth's atmosphere. Pressure halves every ~5.5 km.
Ozone absorption (Chapman cycle):
\(O_2 + h\nu \rightarrow 2O, \quad O + O_2 \rightarrow O_3\)
UV photons (hν) drive ozone formation and destruction, creating the warm stratosphere.

Why It Matters — NGSS

ESS2.D: Weather and Climate. The atmosphere's layered structure regulates Earth's temperature, blocks harmful radiation, enables weather, and protects life. The stratospheric ozone layer blocks UV-B and UV-C radiation — without it, surface DNA damage would make complex life impossible. CFC-driven ozone depletion (Montreal Protocol, 1987) demonstrated humanity's ability to cause and reverse global atmospheric changes. The troposphere's greenhouse effect and its anthropogenic enhancement (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O) is the central challenge of 21st-century Earth science.
