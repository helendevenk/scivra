Common Isotope Systems

Carbon-14 — t½ = 5,730 yrs; range: <50,000 yrs; organic material
Potassium-40 — t½ = 1.25 Ga; rocks >100,000 yrs; volcanic
Uranium-238 — t½ = 4.47 Ga; zircons; oldest Earth rocks
Rubidium-87 — t½ = 49.7 Ga; ancient igneous rocks
Samarium-147 — t½ = 106 Ga; meteorites, oldest minerals

How Half-Life Works

Each half-life, exactly 50% of remaining parent atoms decay into daughter atoms. After 1 half-life: 50% parent remaining. After 2: 25%. After 5: ~3%. After 10 half-lives, only ~0.1% remains — too little to measure accurately. This defines the practical age range of each isotope system.

Assumptions & Uncertainties

Radiometric dating assumes: (1) closed system — no gain/loss of parent or daughter, (2) known initial conditions, (3) constant decay rate (verified by physics). Uncertainties come from mass spectrometer measurement precision, contamination, and system openness. Cross-checking multiple isotopes confirms results.
