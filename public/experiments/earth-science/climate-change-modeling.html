Climate Model Notes

RCP 2.6: Strong mitigation — net CO₂ emissions near zero by 2070. Warming peaks at ~1.5°C. Requires massive renewable energy transition and carbon capture.

RCP 4.5: Moderate mitigation — emissions peak ~2040, then decline. CO₂ stabilizes at ~550 ppm by 2100. Warming ~2.4°C above pre-industrial by 2100.

RCP 8.5: Business as usual — CO₂ reaches ~936 ppm by 2100. Warming 4.3°C (range 3.2–5.4°C). Catastrophic sea level rise, ecosystem collapse, 1 billion climate refugees.

Knowledge Cards
1.5°C vs 2°C Targets
Paris Agreement (2015): limit warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial (preferred) or "well below" 2°C. At 1.5°C: 70% of coral reefs lost. At 2°C: 99% of coral reefs lost. 0.5°C matters enormously for ecosystems.
Carbon Budget
Remaining budget to stay under 1.5°C: ~400 GtCO₂ as of 2023 (~10 years at current emissions of ~40 Gt/yr). For 2°C: ~1150 GtCO₂ (~29 years). Budget shrinks with each passing year of high emissions.
Sea Level Rise
1880–2024: +21 cm total rise. Current rate: 3.7 mm/yr (accelerating). By 2100: RCP 4.5 → +0.4–0.6 m; RCP 8.5 → +0.6–1.1 m. Ice sheet instability could add 1–2 m more.
Why It Matters

Climate models are the scientific basis for all climate policy. The ECS parameter (how sensitive Earth is to CO₂ doubling) has been constrained to 2.5–4°C (IPCC AR6, 2021) — a major advance. Understanding model structure, forcing, feedbacks, and scenario assumptions enables students to critically evaluate climate projections rather than accepting or rejecting them uncritically.
