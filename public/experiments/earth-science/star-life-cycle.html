Stellar Mass Determines Fate

Stars live and die by their mass. A star like our Sun (1 solar mass, M☉) spends ~10 billion years on the main sequence fusing hydrogen into helium. When core hydrogen is exhausted, the core contracts while the outer layers expand — the star becomes a red giant. Low-mass stars (under ~8 M☉) end life as planetary nebulae, ejecting their outer layers while the core becomes a white dwarf — an Earth-sized stellar remnant cooling over billions of years.

Massive stars (above ~8 M☉) live fast and die violently. Their intense gravity drives fusion all the way to iron (Fe-56). Since iron fusion consumes energy rather than releasing it, the core collapses catastrophically in under one second — a core-collapse supernova. The remnant is either a neutron star (ultra-dense, ~20 km diameter, 1.4–3 M☉) or if the original star exceeds ~20–25 M☉, a stellar black hole where gravity overcomes all known forces.

Key Equations
Main Sequence Lifetime:
\(t_{MS} \approx \frac{t_\odot}{(M/M_\odot)^{2.5}}\)
Where t☉ ≈ 10 Gyr for the Sun. A 10 M☉ star lives only ~30 Myr.
Stefan-Boltzmann Luminosity:
\(L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T_{eff}^4\)
Luminosity depends on radius² and temperature⁴ — temperature dominates.
Mass-Luminosity Relation:
\(\frac{L}{L_\odot} \approx \left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)^{3.5}\)
A 10 M☉ star is ~3162× more luminous than the Sun.

Why It Matters — NGSS

ESS1.A: The Universe and Its Stars — stellar nucleosynthesis creates all elements heavier than hydrogen and helium. Every carbon atom in your body was forged in a star that exploded before our solar system formed. Supernovae seed molecular clouds with metals (astronomers call all elements heavier than He "metals"), triggering the next generation of star and planet formation. Understanding stellar evolution is fundamental to cosmochemistry, planetary science, and astrobiology.
