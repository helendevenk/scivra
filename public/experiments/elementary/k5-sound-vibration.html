Sound & Vibration Guitar · Drum · Tuning Fork · K5 Science
Key Formula
For a vibrating string: higher tension or shorter string → higher pitch (more vibrations per second).
A standing wave has nodes (no movement) and antinodes (most movement). More segments = higher frequency!
What's Happening?

When you pluck a guitar string, it vibrates back and forth. The faster it vibrates, the higher the pitch you hear!

A drum membrane vibrates when you hit it. The tighter the drum head, the higher the pitch.

A tuning fork vibrates at exactly one frequency — that's why musicians use it to tune instruments!

Fun Fact
Musical note A4 vibrates at exactly 440 Hz (440 times per second). Piano, guitar, and violin all play the same 440 Hz for this note!
Try This!
Change the harmonic number to see how a string can vibrate in 1, 2, or 3 segments at the same time — these are called harmonics!
Why It Matters
All music is made of vibrations! Engineers design concert halls to have the right shape so sound waves bounce perfectly and everyone can hear clearly.
